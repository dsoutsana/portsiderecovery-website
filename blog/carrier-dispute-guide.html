How to Dispute Carrier Demurrage Charges: A Step-by-Step Guide for Importers

Portside Recovery · June 2026 · 8 min read

You've received a demurrage invoice. It's higher than expected. You suspect the free-time calculation is wrong, or the delay wasn't your fault. Here's exactly how to dispute it — step by step, with carrier-specific filing instructions.

This guide covers the dispute process for the major carriers operating into Australia: Maersk, MSC, CMA CGM, Hapag-Lloyd, ONE, and OOCL.

Before you start: Most carriers require disputes to be filed within 30-90 days of the invoice date. If you're holding invoices older than 90 days, act immediately — some carriers will still accept them with a justification, but your odds drop significantly after the window closes.

Step-by-Step Dispute Process

1

Gather the Core Documents

Before filing anything, collect these four documents. Without them, your dispute won't get past the first review:

  • The invoice itself — showing the charge amount, container number, and invoice date
  • The Bill of Lading (B/L) — confirms the shipment details and original free-time terms
  • The carrier's published tariff — the rate sheet and terms in effect at the time of shipment (not the current one)
  • Terminal gate logs — timestamps showing when the container was discharged, available for pickup, actually picked up, and returned empty

The gate logs are the most important piece of evidence. They're obtained from the terminal operator's Vehicle Booking System (VBS) — DP World's "OneView" or Patrick's "Pathfinder" in Australia. If you use a customs broker, they can pull these for you.

2

Cross-Reference the Invoice Against the Tariff

This is where most disputes are won or lost. Line by line:

  1. Check the free-time start date. It should begin from the container's "available for delivery" timestamp, not the vessel arrival date. A container discharged on a Thursday at 18:00 might not be available until Friday at 08:00. That's one day the carrier often "accidentally" charges.
  2. Count chargeable days manually. Exclude weekends and public holidays in the port jurisdiction. Example: a container available Friday before Easter weekend with pickup on Tuesday — that's 1 chargeable day (Friday), not 5.
  3. Verify the daily rate. Is it the rate from the tariff in effect when the container was shipped? Carriers occasionally apply current rates to old shipments.
  4. Check for rate escalation errors. If the tariff escalates from $150/day to $225/day after day 3, verify the escalation was applied correctly — it's common to see all days billed at the escalated rate.
  5. Look for duplicate charges. The same container billed for both demurrage AND a "storage fee" or "terminal fee" that covers the same period.
3

Identify the Grounds for Dispute

Your dispute needs a specific ground. "The charge seems high" won't work. Valid grounds include:

  • Incorrect free-time calculation — holidays not excluded, wrong start date
  • Terminal congestion delay — container was available but no VBS slots were open
  • Carrier-caused delay — carrier redirected the container, changed the return depot, or their systems were down
  • Rate mismatch — invoice uses a rate not in the applicable tariff
  • Force majeure — port shutdown, industrial action, or natural disaster during the charge period
  • Duplicate billing — same container, same period, charged twice under different line items

Reference the specific tariff clause, the specific date, and attach the gate logs as evidence. A dispute that says "Clause 4.2 of your 2025 tariff excludes public holidays; 26 January was a public holiday at Port Botany; the invoice incorrectly counts this as a chargeable day" will get approved. A vague complaint won't.

4

File Through the Carrier's Dispute Portal

Each carrier has a specific portal and process. Here's where to go:

  • Maersk: MyFinance portal → Dispute Center → "Create Dispute" → select invoice → upload evidence
  • MSC: myMSC → eBusiness → Invoice Disputes → attach tariff reference and gate logs
  • CMA CGM: CMA CGM eBusiness → Finance → Dispute Management → requires container number + invoice number
  • Hapag-Lloyd: Online Business Suite → Invoices → select invoice → "Raise Dispute" → note tariff clause
  • ONE (Ocean Network Express): ONE eCommerce → Dispute Center → "Create New Dispute" → upload PDF evidence pack
  • OOCL: MyOOCL Center → Finance → Invoice Dispute → attach B/L + tariff + gate logs

All carriers accept PDF attachments. Compile your evidence into a single PDF with a cover page summarising the dispute grounds in 2-3 bullet points. Carriers' dispute teams process hundreds of claims daily — make yours the easiest one on their desk.

5

Track and Escalate

Most carriers acknowledge disputes within 2-5 business days and resolve within 14-30 days. If you don't hear back:

  • Day 7: Follow up through the portal with a reference to your original dispute ID
  • Day 14: Email the carrier's Australian finance team directly (addresses are in the tariff document)
  • Day 30: Escalate to the carrier's regional disputes manager. Reference the dispute ID, the tariff clause, and note the time elapsed without resolution

Australian consumer law applies to B2B service contracts. If a carrier ignores a valid dispute for 30+ days, you have grounds for a formal complaint through the ACCC — though this is rarely necessary. Most carriers resolve once reminded.

What If the Dispute Is Rejected?

Rejection isn't final. Carriers often reject first-round disputes with a generic response. If rejected:

  1. Request the specific reason in writing — quoting the tariff clause they're relying on to uphold the charge
  2. Check their response against the tariff. If they cite a clause that doesn't apply to your shipment dates, point it out
  3. Re-file with additional evidence — terminal operator records, email correspondence, or booking confirmations that strengthen your case
  4. Escalate internally. Request review by a senior disputes officer, not the first-line processor

In our experience, 40% of initially rejected disputes are approved on re-filing when stronger evidence is provided.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Disputing without the tariff. Without quoting the carrier's own tariff clause, you're asking for a favour — not filing a dispute.
  • Filing through the wrong channel. Customer service and finance are different teams. Disputes filed to customer service sit unprocessed.
  • Missing the deadline. Every carrier has a dispute window. Find it in the tariff and file before it closes.
  • Emotional language. "This charge is unfair" gets ignored. "Clause 4.2 of Tariff 2025-Q3 excludes the public holiday on 26 January; therefore 2 days are incorrectly charged" gets resolved.
  • Not tracking the dispute. Disputes fall through cracks. Set calendar reminders for the 7-day, 14-day, and 30-day follow-up points.
