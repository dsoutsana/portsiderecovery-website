Demurrage and Detention Charges Explained: What Australian Importers Need to Know

Portside Recovery · June 2026 · 6 min read

If you import containers into Australia, you've seen the invoices. A shipment arrives, clears customs, gets delivered — and two weeks later an invoice arrives from the shipping line for $1,800 in "demurrage" or "detention" charges. No explanation. No breakdown. Just a number and a due date.

You're not alone. Demurrage and detention charges represent one of the largest hidden costs in Australian importing — and in many cases, they're contestable. Here's what they actually mean and how to push back.

What's the Difference Between Demurrage and Detention?

Though often lumped together on invoices, demurrage and detention are legally distinct charges covering different stages of the container lifecycle.

Demurrage

Demurrage applies when a full import container sits at the terminal beyond the free-time period after discharge from the vessel. Free time at Australian ports typically ranges from 3 to 7 days depending on the carrier and terminal (DP World, Patrick, VICT, etc.). Once that window closes, daily charges begin — anywhere from $150 to $350 per day for a standard 20' or 40' container.

Detention

Detention applies when a container is picked up from the terminal but not returned empty within the allowed window. This is the "street" period — the container is in your possession. Free detention time varies by carrier but is commonly 7 to 10 days. Daily rates are similar to demurrage.

Key point: Demurrage = container sitting at the port. Detention = container sitting at your warehouse. The same container can incur both charges if delayed at both ends.

How Are These Charges Calculated?

Each carrier publishes a tariff — a legal document specifying free-time allowances, daily rates, and escalation schedules. These tariffs are not standardised. Maersk, MSC, CMA CGM, Hapag-Lloyd, ONE, and OOCL each have their own. A container that incurs $600 in charges under one carrier's tariff might incur $2,400 under another's for the same delay.

Tariffs typically escalate over time. Day 1-3 might be $150/day, days 4-7 $225/day, and days 8+ $350/day. The escalation penalises importers who don't realise they're accruing charges until the invoice arrives.

When Are These Charges Contestable?

Carriers rely on importers paying without question. But many charges are disputable on several grounds:

1. Terminal Delays Not Caused by the Importer

If the terminal itself caused the delay — congestion, equipment breakdown, labour shortages, or a slot-booking system that had no availability — the charge isn't your fault. Australian terminals experienced significant congestion through 2024-2026, and carriers frequently charged importers for delays the terminal caused.

2. Public Holidays and Port Closures

Most carrier tariffs exclude weekends and public holidays from free-time calculations — but they don't always apply these exclusions correctly. A container that arrived the Wednesday before Easter might be billed as if Thursday-Monday were chargeable days when only one of those days should count.

3. Carrier-Caused Detention

If the carrier's own systems prevented timely return — a Booking System down, no empty-container acceptance at the nominated depot, or a last-minute change to the return location — the detention charge is contestable under the carrier's own terms.

4. Tariff Calculation Errors

Simple arithmetic mistakes are surprisingly common. A gate-out timestamp recorded as 09:15 might be billed as if it occurred at 00:01 the previous day. A container returned at 23:45 might be billed for an extra day because the system rounds to midnight. These errors are worth hundreds of dollars each.

What Can You Actually Recover?

In our experience auditing Australian import invoices, 40-60% of demurrage and detention charges have at least one contestable element. The average recovery per dispute ranges from $500 to $5,000 depending on container volume and the complexity of the carrier's tariff.

For an importer running 20 containers per month, that's $6,000-$60,000 per year in potentially recoverable charges — using the carrier's own tariff rules against them.

Common Questions

Do I need a lawyer to dispute these charges?

No. Demurrage and detention disputes are administrative — they're filed through the carrier's own claims portal using their published tariff as the basis. You don't need legal representation. You need someone who understands carrier tariffs and knows which evidence to cite.

Will disputing damage my relationship with the carrier?

Carriers expect disputes. They build a dispute rate into their revenue models. Filing a tariff-backed dispute on valid grounds does not affect your service agreements, credit terms, or booking priority.

How far back can I dispute?

Most carriers allow disputes within 30-90 days of the invoice date, though some accept claims up to 12 months. The sooner you act, the better your position — terminal records and gate timestamps are easier to obtain for recent shipments.

What evidence do I need?

At minimum: the invoice itself, the bill of lading, and the carrier's published tariff for the relevant period. In stronger cases: terminal gate logs, booking confirmations, and correspondence showing delays outside your control.
