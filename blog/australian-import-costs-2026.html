The Hidden Cost of Container Imports: Australian Port Charges in 2026

Portside Recovery · June 2026 · 7 min read

When importers budget for a container shipment, they focus on the freight rate. But the ocean freight is only the beginning. Port charges, terminal handling fees, carrier surcharges, quarantine inspection costs, and infrastructure levies — collectively known as port ancillary charges — can add 15-30% to the landed cost of every container. And unlike the freight rate, these charges are rarely negotiated.

Here's a breakdown of what Australian importers actually pay at the port gate in 2026 — and what's worth pushing back on.

On a single container, these charges can total $1,700-$3,400 before demurrage or detention even enters the picture. Run 10 containers a month, and you're looking at $17,000-$34,000 in port charges alone.

Which Charges Are Actually Disputable?

Not all port charges are negotiable — but several categories are worth questioning:

Container Cleaning Fees

Carriers routinely charge cleaning fees claiming the container was returned dirty. But under most carrier terms, "broom-clean" is the standard — not "steam-cleaned." If your container was returned empty with no residue, a $150 cleaning fee is contestable. Demand photo evidence. Most carriers won't provide it.

Congestion Surcharges

If the terminal was congested — not your supply chain — the congestion is the carrier's operational risk, not yours. Carriers apply congestion surcharges broadly, but if the delay was at their nominated terminal and you had a valid slot booking, the charge should be disputed.

Demurrage and Detention (the Big One)

These are the single largest recoverable cost category for most importers. As covered in detail in our demurrage and detention guide, 40-60% of these charges have contestable elements when cross-referenced against the carrier's own published tariff.

Tip: If you're importing through multiple ports, negotiate cartage rates per-port rather than accepting a blended rate. The savings on a Brisbane vs. Sydney cartage lane alone can be $150 per container.

Three Things Importers Can Do Right Now

  1. Audit your last 12 months of port charges. Pull every terminal invoice and carrier surcharge from the past year. Categorise by type. You'll likely find cleaning fees on containers you know were returned clean, and demurrage charges on containers that sat at the terminal through no fault of yours.
  2. Request the carrier's published tariff. Under Australian consumer law, the tariff is the contract. If the invoice doesn't match the tariff — wrong free-time calculation, incorrect daily rate, holidays not excluded — you have grounds for dispute.
  3. Negotiate THCs on your next contract. Terminal handling charges are one of the few port costs that carriers will negotiate — but only if you ask. Even a $40 reduction per container on 200 containers a year is $8,000 in saved costs.
